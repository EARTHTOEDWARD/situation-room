# How to Play — Situation Room: The Burr

## Players and time

- **1–5 human players**; rules-based NPCs fill unused roles
- six rounds; human completion time has not yet been measured in a playtest
- one shared laptop or tablet
- optional phones or paper for private notes

The five roles are Belarus, Russia, the United States, the European Union, and the United Nations.

## Objective

There is no single conquest score. At the end, each role is judged on four coupled outcomes:

1. core interests secured;
2. leadership or institutional survival;
3. national or constituency welfare;
4. credibility and future leverage.

The whole table also receives a **world viability** score. A state can achieve a narrow strategic objective while collectively producing a disastrous result.

## What is public and private

### Public board

Everyone can see:

- the escalation ladder;
- media heat;
- Russia–Belarus entanglement;
- selected expiring structural clocks;
- public claims and visible actions;
- formal commitments and the public crisis log.

### Private situation room

Only the active player should see:

- true stake temperatures;
- sacrifice ceiling;
- exact leadership, welfare, credibility, and wiggle room;
- faction pressure;
- all four role assets;
- private inbox;
- the action plan before it is sealed.

The privacy screen is social rather than cryptographic. Other players should look away during a handoff.

## Set up

Choose one of four presets:

- **Solo command:** one human role and four NPCs; the United Nations is selected initially, but you can choose any role.
- **Five-player table:** all roles human.
- **Two-player quick start:** Belarus and Russia human; the other three roles use NPCs.
- **Observer:** all roles use NPCs.

You may change any individual seat between human and NPC. Enter a replay seed to make engine randomness reproducible.

The NPCs are deterministic, scenario-specific decision policies running entirely in the browser. They carry the changing ladder, commitments, faction pressure, and asset clocks forward; they do not use an external language model or parse free-form conversation.

## Each round

### 1. Open negotiation or solo review

At a human table, players may speak openly or step away for private discussion. In Solo command, read the public board and your role’s inbox for the signals left by earlier rounds. Verbal or imagined promises are non-binding until encoded through actions.

### 2. Sealed private planning

Press **Begin sealed planning** and pass the device to each human role.

Every role chooses:

- **one internal faction** to privilege;
- **one public action**;
- **one private action**, with a target when required.

NPC choices are generated before human planning begins and remain sealed.

### 3. Simultaneous resolution

After all human plans are sealed, the engine resolves:

- public escalation and de-escalation pressure;
- matching private intentions;
- proxy restraint or covert support;
- intelligence and quiet-room probes;
- faction pressure and room events;
- hollow public claims;
- asset-clock decay;
- secret-agreement leaks;
- welfare and leadership damage.

### 4. Carry the structure forward

The ladder may return to an earlier rung, but the crisis does not reset. Public guarantees, depleted assets, audience costs, faction pressure, secret annexes, and entanglement remain.

## The three stake temperatures

- **Negotiable:** useful instrument; can be traded without threatening the actor’s identity or survival.
- **Vital:** expensive to concede; requires a serious compensating offer.
- **Existential:** perceived loss threatens leadership, regime, constitutional order, or survival.

A player’s public claim can run hotter than the true stake. That can deter an opponent, but repeated overclaiming produces **bluff fatigue**.

## Bluffing and sell-by dates

A public claim expires after two rounds unless backed by costly action. An unbacked expiry becomes a hollow threat and damages credibility.

Some leverage also decays structurally:

- Belarusian incident deniability erodes as evidence accumulates;
- Russia’s mobilisation lead decays as opponents catch up;
- EU sanctions unity decays when threats remain unresolved;
- US reinforcement options close;
- UN quiet channels close under media saturation.

## Internal factions

Every room has three functional pressures:

- **Hawks:** deterrence, punishment, readiness, reputation.
- **Statesmen:** optionality, verification, sequencing, reversible moves.
- **Survival coalition:** the people or institutions that keep the leadership in office.

Privileging one faction relieves it. Ignored factions may reach pressure 3 and generate a room event: leaks, resignations, unauthorised escalation, coalition withdrawal, or elite challenge.

Authoritarian roles pay lower immediate public audience costs than democratic or institutional roles, but hidden elite challenges can threaten leadership abruptly.

## Secret diplomacy and face-saving

Private actions can match in the same round: restraint can be exchanged for conditional protection, demobilisation for a deployment assurance, or delayed sanctions for a verified inquiry. The UN can supply escrow, inspection, or a secret annex.

UN mechanisms can let both sides move simultaneously and can reopen a welded rung. Secret concessions avoid immediate public cost but may later leak.

## Worked mechanism example

Suppose one actor publicly overclaims a negotiable issue while privately offering restraint in exchange for protection. The bargain forms only if the intended counterpart independently chooses the matching conditional offer in the same round. A match can lower the ladder while increasing entanglement and creating a classified concession with future leak risk.

A second actor’s simultaneous private feeler does **not** automatically become a deal if its counterpart used its single private action elsewhere. An unmatched feeler is still information: the next round begins with a new private signal but without a completed bargain.

## End of game

The game ends after six resolved rounds or earlier if the ladder reaches nuclear use, a major leadership loses control, or a durable settlement contains the crisis.

The debrief reveals:

- true stakes;
- overclaims and hollow expiries;
- secret commitments and leaks;
- internal faction events;
- role scores;
- shared world viability.

## Solo-mode evidence boundary

Automated solo-path checks show that a one-human-seat game can complete the six-round state/action loop, that the human-seat choice can make or break a matching bargain, and that the engine carries consequences forward reproducibly. This remains a synthetic diagnostic until a person completes a timed solo playtest; it is not evidence of human bargaining quality, strategic intelligence, game balance, or real-world geopolitical prediction.
