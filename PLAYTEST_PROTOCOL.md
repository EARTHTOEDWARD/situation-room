# First Human Playtest Protocol

## Active milestone

Determine whether **The Burr** produces genuine proxy–patron entrapment and politically constrained bargaining without the internal-faction layer overwhelming play.

## Minimum session

- five human players if possible;
- one facilitator who does not advise strategy;
- six rounds;
- export the replay JSON at the end;
- 10-minute post-game discussion.

A two-player Belarus–Russia session with AI support is useful as a bridge test, but it does not replace the five-player witness.

## Do not explain before play

Beyond the built-in **How to Play** and **Show Me a Round**, do not explain:

- Belarus’s hidden stake hierarchy;
- which private actions match;
- the intended settlement;
- how authoritarian and democratic constraints are expected to differ.

The game should surface these through play.

## Record during play

The replay already stores actions, ladder, media, entanglement, deals, and final scores. The facilitator should additionally note:

1. moments when a player says or implies, “I cannot say that publicly”;
2. any rule or card that must be explained twice;
3. whether players negotiate before pressing **Begin sealed planning**;
4. whether Belarus believes it can act independently of Russia;
5. whether Russia feels entrapped, abandoned, or in control;
6. whether the UN creates an exit or merely reacts to one;
7. any round that feels administratively slow.

## Post-game questions

Ask each player independently:

1. What was your real highest-priority stake?
2. Which opponent did you misread most?
3. Which concession could you accept privately but not publicly?
4. Did your internal factions change an action you otherwise wanted to take?
5. Which asset clock altered your timing?
6. Did a proxy or ally force you into a commitment you did not initially want?
7. What single rule would you remove?
8. What real-world tension was missing?

## Decision rule

### Proceed to networked private clients when

- at least two of three sessions produce a non-scripted Belarus–Russia entrapment or restraint path;
- most players can complete round two without rule help;
- at least one viable agreement is identified before it becomes publicly implementable;
- internal faction events are consequential but not the dominant source of confusion.

### Revise the vertical slice first when

- players choose actions by card text rather than a model of other actors;
- the same bargain forms every session;
- table talk does not alter plans;
- faction pressure requires repeated bookkeeping explanation;
- the UN role lacks independent agency;
- public/private secrecy is routinely broken by the single-device format.
