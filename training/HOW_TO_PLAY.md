# How to Play — Conviction: Existential

## The point of the game

You are a UN mediator. You do not command either principal. Your instrument is judgement.

Each side has hidden **stake temperatures**:

- **NEG — Negotiable:** expendable, tradable, or primarily instrumental.
- **VIT — Vital:** costly to surrender and capable of driving major escalation.
- **EXI — Existential:** treated as a threat to regime, state, or identity survival.

Public declarations can be honest or theatrical. Your final calibration score compares your private dropdown choices with the hidden truth. Correctly detecting an exaggerated claim is especially valuable.

## What you are looking at

### The two dossiers

Each stake row contains:

1. a public-claim chip (`NEG`, `VIT`, or `EXI`); and
2. your own assessment dropdown (`?`, `NEG`, `VIT`, or `EXI`).

The public claim is evidence, not truth. The dropdown is your model of the principal.

### The escalation ladder

The centre column is the crisis state, from normal relations to nuclear use. As the principals climb, rungs above mid-temperature war can weld behind them. A welded rung cannot be crossed on the way down without a face-saving token.

### Credibility and face-saving tokens

- Probe / shuttle diplomacy: **−10 credibility**.
- Failed mediation based on the wrong wound: **−5 credibility**.
- Successful climb-down: **+8 credibility**.
- Mint one face-saving token: **−25 credibility**.

## A turn

1. **Read:** compare claims, welfare, fusion, ladder position, and the situation log.
2. **Probe:** choose a principal and obtain one behavioural signal.
3. **Revise:** update the appropriate assessment dropdowns.
4. **Broker or wait:** try a climb-down when you have a theory of the hotter party's wound; otherwise advance.
5. **Advance:** your current assessments are recorded and the principals act.

Repeat until the crisis reaches normal relations, nuclear use, a regime falls, or the eight-turn window closes.

## Worked example — not a Gulf 2026 spoiler

Imagine a fictional principal declares:

> “Control of Port Delta is existential.”

You should not simply select `EXI`.

1. You probe the principal. Its leaders accept significant commercial losses but mobilise only partially. The signal reads **VITAL**, not existential.
2. You set **Port Delta → VIT** in your dropdown.
3. You attempt mediation with a ceremonial communiqué. The offer is rejected because it is merely symbolic.
4. You then offer a verifiable security guarantee. The principal accepts a one-rung climb-down.
5. You update your model: the port is probably vital, while the underlying security guarantee may touch an existential concern.

The lesson is not “always distrust claims.” It is: **use behaviour, accepted costs, and response to concessions to distinguish the stated object from the underlying wound.**

## A recommended first play

Turn 1:

1. Scan both dossiers.
2. Make provisional assessments; leaving some rows as `?` is legitimate.
3. Probe one principal.
4. Update the row named in the signal.
5. Advance the turn.

Turn 2 onward:

- Compare the new ladder movement and situation log with your theory.
- Probe the other side or revisit a doubtful stake.
- Broker only when you can state which party must be moved and why the proposed concession matters to that party.
- Preserve at least one face-saving token if the ladder is approaching rung 6.

## Common mistakes

- **Treating every existential claim as true.** Public rhetoric can be an audience trap or bluff.
- **Assuming peace alone wins.** You can de-escalate by luck and still score poorly if your reads are wrong.
- **Repeating a rejected concession.** A rejection is evidence that the offer missed the hotter party's wound.
- **Spending every token early.** Tokens matter only when the ratchet has welded.
- **Advancing without updating dropdowns.** Your current reads are recorded each turn.

- `P` — probe / shuttle
- `B` — broker climb-down
- `M` — mint face-saving token
- `Enter` — advance turn
- `Esc` — close a dialog or exit the walkthrough
- `←` / `→` — move through the walkthrough
